# mostyn-read-scopes — analysis

*Source: ft Darren Mostyn, 7K views · framesRead: 24*

## Overview
This is a SCOPE-READING masterclass, not a step-by-step grade walkthrough. The whole video is shot on a Flanders Scientific reference suite driving DaVinci Resolve through a Blackmagic Advanced/Mini panel, and the scopes are NOT Resolve's built-in scopes — Mostyn runs OmniScope (the FSI hardware/software scope engine) on a 32" ultrawide because it is far more customizable and gives him scopes Resolve doesn't have. His central thesis: scopes are a GUIDE, not the grade ("I'm not grading by scopes" — f018). He uses them to verify exposure and balance, then makes creative decisions by eye on the calibrated reference monitor. The four scopes he teaches in depth: (1) false color for exposure, (2) the vectorscope skin-tone line, used in conjunction with false color, (3) "Twin Peaks" — a 3D brightness/hue/saturation scope unique to OmniScope, used for white-balance and saturation sanity, and (4) a shadow-only RGB parade (bottom 10/15/20% IRE) for detailed black balancing and clip detection. He stresses scopes should be dimmed (low gain) so you read level, not brightness, and can be soloed/isolated to a region (e.g. just a face). He repeatedly proves his targets against film references (Top Gun: Maverick, Her, a desert/motel film) to show real cinema skin tones sit BELOW the vectorscope skin line and below the 55–65 IRE "highlight" zone. The footage shown is "color managed but not graded yet" (f016) — so RCM is implied as the working pipeline, though no project-settings panel is ever opened.

## Order of operations
1. 1. Set up scopes for reading, not looking: dim the scope GAIN way down (Settings > gain) so you read LEVEL not brightness; lay them out in a saved custom preset; size each scope by clicking it (f005-f006).
2. 2. SOLO / ISOLATE before measuring: right-click a scope to solo it; isolate a region (e.g. lock onto just the subject's face) so every scope reads only the skin, not the whole frame (f007).
3. 3. EXPOSURE FIRST with false color: use false color to get skin into the right IRE ballpark before touching color — 'use my false color first to get me in the right ballpark in terms of exposure' (f012).
4. 4. THEN balance/skin with the vectorscope skin-tone line: switch to the skin-tone indicator (analyzes the skin line) and check whether skin sits on / under / over the line; keep it CONSISTENT across shots (f013, f016).
5. 5. Treat false color + skin line as a combined GUIDE only — once exposure is parked, the rest is a creative decision by eye (f015, f018).
6. 6. Verify against references: pull up film stills (Top Gun: Maverick, Her) and confirm your targets match real cinema (skin slightly green/under the line, not in the 55-65 gray) (f020-f028).
7. 7. White-balance / saturation sanity-check with Twin Peaks: read the 3D brightness-hue-saturation scope — straighter central line = more neutral white balance; not splaying too far left/right = saturation not overcooked (f026-f038).
8. 8. Detailed SHADOW work last, in the log/shadow wheels: switch to the bottom-15% RGB shadow parade, line up R-G-B in the blacks with the LOG tool, watch for channel clipping, then optionally push a deliberate color INTO the shadows (e.g. pull red down for cooler blacks) (f042-f046).

## Scopes
- **False color (exposure)** — Overlay the false-color map on the image with an IRE scale running down the side; green = his target skin zone, gray = highlights. He uses the built-in Flanders false-color preset (multiple charts available, e.g. RE-1). Read where the colored patches fall on the skin against the side scale. Seen as the blue/cyan/green map insets (f011, f013, f017). · targets: Skin exposure ~35 IRE at its lowest up to 55-60, peaking ~70 only on a forehead/cheek highlight; the 55-65 'gray' band on the Flanders chart is HIGHLIGHTS, so keep the bulk of the face BELOW it (around 40-50). 'Majority of it's round about 50 IRE, maybe a little bit less' (f015).
- **Vectorscope + skin-tone line** — OmniScope skin-tone indicator analyzes the famous I/Q skin line. Soloed to the face, the trace shows whether skin leans green (under line) or yellow (warmer/toward line). Warming the image visibly rotates the trace from green toward yellow toward the line (f013-f014, f017). Pointed at directly with a pen at f021/f028. · targets: Skin should sit ON, UNDER, or OVER the skin line but most importantly CONSISTENTLY in the same relation across all shots. He prefers skin slightly UNDER the line (reads slightly green) — and shows Top Gun/Her do the same. 'You just have to be consistently under, on it, or over it' (f022).
- **Twin Peaks (3D brightness / hue / saturation)** — A double-diamond scope unique to OmniScope (not in Resolve). Vertical axis = luminance (black center → peak white at tips). With saturation removed it collapses to a single straight line. Hue wraps around; pushing the grade red/blue/green swings the whole trace into that quadrant (f037). Used as a quick white-balance + saturation gut-check (f032, f034, f036). · targets: Central vertical line as STRAIGHT as possible for neutral white balance (black at center, peak white at the top tip); trace not splaying too far left/right = saturation under control; lobes/petals show dominant hues (e.g. 'a lean towards blue' = sky) (f028-f038).
- **Shadow-only RGB parade (bottom 10/15/20% IRE)** — Essentially a normal RGB parade waveform but zoomed to only the bottom 10/15/20% IRE — a 'bullseye view into the shadows.' Settable percentage. He bumps the scope gain up temporarily to read it clearly, then adjusts with the LOG tool / shadow wheels and watches which channel clips first (green clipped in his demo, f034). His single favorite for detailed shadow work. · targets: Line up the R, G, B channels in the blacks for a clean neutral black; watch for any single channel CLIPPING; or deliberately offset a channel to tint the shadows (pull red down = cooler/bluer blacks) (f042-f046).
- **RGB parade + RGB histogram + standard vectorscope (supporting)** — Standard tools always present in his layout (f003 shows full layout: RGB parade up top, luma waveform, RGB histogram bottom-left, standard vectorscope right, plus three quadrant mini-vectorscopes split into shadow/midtone/highlight reading the bottom 15% of shadows in R/G/B — f003, f046). · targets: General brightness/balance per channel; he says he could lose the histogram and the timecode before anything else (f039-f040).

## Skin tone
The skin-tone-line method is the heart of the video. He uses the vectorscope's I-axis skin line via OmniScope's skin-tone indicator, ALWAYS in conjunction with false color: false color sets exposure first, then the skin line confirms hue balance. His contrarian teaching point: real cinema skin does NOT sit on the line — it sits slightly UNDER it (reading slightly green), and that 'doesn't mean it looks green, it just means it's sitting balanced under the skin tone line' (f019). He proves it on Top Gun: Maverick (f020-f022, skin under the line, exposure 40-50 dropping to 30, only a cheek hitting 60) and Her (f023-f025). The rule he wants people to unlearn: 'People think you have to sit on that line. You just have to be consistently under, on it, or over it' (f028). Consistency across shots matters more than the absolute position. Warming the subject visibly rotates the skin trace from green toward yellow/the line (f014, f017).

## Teaching points
- Scopes are a GUIDE, not the grade. Park exposure and balance with scopes, then make the look a creative decision by EYE on a calibrated monitor — 'I'm not grading by scopes' (f015/f018). A beginner's biggest mistake is chasing the trace instead of trusting their eyes.
- Dim your scopes. Turn scope GAIN down so you read LEVEL, not brightness — bright scopes distract; you only need to see where the trace sits (f005).
- Order matters: EXPOSURE before COLOR. False color first to land skin in the right IRE ballpark, THEN the vectorscope skin line for hue balance. Don't balance color on a wrongly-exposed face (f012).
- Skin does NOT have to sit ON the vectorscope skin line. Real cinema skin sits slightly UNDER it (reads slightly green) and still looks perfect; what matters is being CONSISTENT (under/on/over) across every shot in the project (f019/f022/f028).
- The 55-65 IRE 'gray' band is HIGHLIGHTS, not where skin lives. Put the bulk of the face around 40-50 IRE and let only small highlights climb toward 60-70 (f018).
- Isolate before you measure. Solo a scope and lock onto just the face so the trace reads only the skin, free of background contamination (f007).
- Reference real films. Pull stills from movies you admire and read THEIR scopes to calibrate your own targets — it proves the rules and kills myths (Top Gun, Her — f020-f028).
- For black work, zoom the parade to just the bottom 10-15-20% — a 'bullseye' on the shadows. Line up R/G/B for neutral blacks, watch for the first channel to clip, or deliberately offset one channel to tint the shadows (cooler blacks = pull red down) using the LOG wheels (f035/f036/f046).
- OmniScope > stock Resolve scopes for this colorist: customizable layout, soloing, region isolation, false-color charts, the Twin Peaks 3D scope, and shadow-band parades that Resolve doesn't natively offer — while reading the exact same 'true' trace Resolve outputs.
- Twin Peaks scope reads brightness + hue + saturation at once: straighter central line = more neutral white balance; trace not splaying far left/right = saturation not overcooked. A fast pre-grade sanity check (f026-f038).
