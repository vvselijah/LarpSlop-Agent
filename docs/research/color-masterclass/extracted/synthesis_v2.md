## 1. The pivot is the load-bearing contrast knob — and 0.18 is correct for LINEAR, NOT for the look LUTs

The single strongest cross-video consensus is that **contrast is a pivoted operation and the pivot location IS the creative move**, not a fixed constant:

- Mullins proves the demonstrated working pivot for **18% grey lands at 0.435 in log/DWG, NOT 0.5** with a `MONO-MIDDLE-GRAY` DCTL read straight off the waveform (mullins-grading-philosophy, 21:05–21:52), and shows **low pivot (0.374) protects highlights / high pivot (0.934) protects shadows** at the same contrast 1.724 (20:34, 20:41).
- The 2hr beginner course raises pivot 0.435→**0.702** specifically "to protect highlights while darkening mids" (2hr-beginner-course, 12:11).
- `grading-too-complicated` is explicit that **pivot is working-space-dependent**: "in DaVinci Wide Gamut set Pivot to 0.336" because 0.435 is wrong for DWG (08:26); and the print-DRT (`MONO-LAB`) pivots at **0.391** (02:59).

The new corpus re-confirms 0.435 as the **DWG-encoded default pivot** across every Resolve build (lenz-most-important-concept 08:24/20:33; wampus-pro-seminar 01:16/02:18; euro-pro-level-64min 03:09; jenkinson-exact-system 00:23; qazi-start-here 00:03; batal-conquer-color-page 00:21; neistadt-ultimate-guide 00:02; faris-full-course-2026 00:24; content-creators-indepth 19:54; benkabouche-1h-master 06:53), and benkabouche reads the **linear** 0.18 anchor straight off the Log3G10 / V-Log specs (18% grey = 0.18 linear / code 433, 16:01–16:04, 10:03) — independent confirmation that colorkit's linear 0.18 and the UI's encoded 0.435 are the *same* point in different spaces. The **pivot-bias-as-creative-move** is now demonstrated outright: Jenkinson adds gentle contrast at the default pivot (Contrast 1.058 @ Pivot 0.435) but for a **stronger** look **lowers the pivot to 0.337** (jenkinson-exact-system 27:53, 36:38), and the working contrast range lands at **1.16–1.18 @ 0.435** on euro-pro-level-64min (21:34, 22:18, 24:44). Qazi runs contrast through an **S-curve by default** ("Use S-curve for contrast" ON, qazi-start-here 05:53).

**Verdict for colorkit:** colorkit's develop is correct — `pivoted_contrast(L, c, 0.18)` pivots on **scene-linear** 18% grey, which is the right anchor *in linear light* (0.435 is the same point *after a log/2.4 encode*, which colorkit never grades in). So **keep 0.18 in `tonemap.pivoted_contrast`**. The gap is that **pivot and contrast amount are hard-coded** (`_DEFAULT_CONTRAST=1.08`, pivot fixed at `_MID`). Demonstrated working contrast is **scene-adaptive 1.0–1.18 for clean footage** (qualifier-tricks 02:09 = 1.104; beginners-guide 1.036–1.052; jenkinson 1.058–1.130; euro-pro 1.16–1.18) and the **highlight-vs-shadow protection bias is a pivot move you cannot get any other way** (Jenkinson 0.435→0.337 for stronger contrast). → Expose `contrast` and a linear-pivot bias as tunable params; default unchanged so the approved look is byte-identical. (P2.)

## 2. Skin is the product. Build a real skin-tone-line solver — this is the #1 missing capability.

Every single colorist video gates the grade on skin, and they converge on a concrete, implementable method that colorkit has **zero** of today (`scene_stats` only counts skin *fraction*, never measures or steers its hue/exposure):

- **Skin hue target = the ~123° vectorscope I-line** (cullen-master-scopes 03:20/15:43; mostyn-read-scopes 00:42/01:35; cullen-36 25:50). The new corpus unanimously confirms this as the skin/WB authority via Resolve's built-in **"Show Skin Tone Indicator" I-bar at ~11 o'clock** (jenkinson-exact-system 26:55, 30:12, 33:00; neistadt-ultimate-guide 27:25, 38:28–38:37; qazi-start-here 19:39; editing-explained-2hr 49:08/71:04/99:52). Measured as `atan2(b*, a*)` on masked-skin pixels in Lab.
- **But do NOT hard-snap to it** — this is the demonstrated refinement over the generic "put skin on the line" theory. Cullen judges skin **by eye, rejecting the 123° snap as "an arbitrary metric"** (cullen-contrast 11:40). Mostyn: skin can sit **on / under / over the line — "as long as it's consistent"**, and film references trend **slightly green of the I-line** and still read natural (mostyn-read-scopes 05:37–05:42, 04:55). `grading-too-complicated` quantifies the sweet spot: push until the skin heatmap turns **yellow = on the line**, past that **magenta = overshot** (07:20–07:22). The new corpus echoes "guideline, not target": Wampus says **"the vectorscope skin-tone line is a guideline, not a hard target"** (wampus-pro-seminar 69:38), and Neistadt warns **don't push skin to a "Hulk" extreme** (neistadt-ultimate-guide 37:56).
- **Skin EXPOSURE is a two-band model in IRE** (the most precise demonstrated numbers, mostyn-read-scopes): midtone bulk **40–50 IRE**, highlights **55–65 IRE**, **hard ceiling ~70 IRE** (02:21, 02:40, 04:35, 05:21). And **measure the skin ROI, not the whole frame** — a bright background fools the eye and the mean (04:22–04:31). Corroborated by Neistadt's eyedropper-built skin key reading **Lum Low ~55–60 / High ~70** on the 0–100 qualifier scale (neistadt-ultimate-guide 36:37, 38:09), and Jenkinson's default scope reference levels Low 20 / High 65 (jenkinson-exact-system 07:59).
- **Expose first, color second** (mostyn-read-scopes 02:57; cullen-contrast pipeline-before-primary). colorkit's develop already exposes via median→0.18 globally; the upgrade is a **skin-gated** exposure/tint nudge layered after the global develop.
- The demonstrated HSL skin key is **hue center ~32° (0–100 / ~123° vectorscope), width ~14–22, luma 54–63%, soft edges** (2hr-beginner-course 21:38, 38:36; qualifier-tricks 04:42–05:33). The new corpus pins it even tighter: an **eyedropper-built skin key of Hue Center 37.9, Width 4.4, Sat 3–8, Lum 60–70, with Matte-Finesse cleanup (Clean Black/White, Blur Radius) rather than tightening the bounds** (neistadt-ultimate-guide 36:37, 38:09) — directly reusable as a soft mask spec.

**Engine recipe (new `measure.skin_signature` + a skin stage in `correct.py`):** mask skin (the existing YCrCb rule already in `scene_stats`, tightened with a luma gate), compute `(mean L in IRE, hue angle vs 123°, chroma)`; then a *small, capped* correction: lift/lower the skin band toward 40–65 IRE, rotate skin hue toward the I-line **with a tunable offset that allows a slightly-green filmic resting point**, never a hard snap. This is the **80%-of-the-look-in-two-moves** that Cullen names (cullen-contrast 12:19): separation + skin-safe balance. **P0** — it is the highest-leverage change for this creator's footage and nothing else in the engine touches skin.

## 3. White balance: the demonstrated method is *constrained* balance (maximize separation s.t. skin doesn't get worse), not just gray-world

colorkit uses Shades-of-Gray (Minkowski p=6) WB, faded in low light — a reasonable robust illuminant estimate. But the pros frame balance as a **two-term optimization**, not a one-shot gray estimate:

- Cullen's "Two Keys": **(1) maximize vectorscope hue separation** (push aggressively — "I can always kill it later"), **(2) skin must get better or at least no worse** (cullen-contrast 09:57–11:30). This is a *constraint*, and it's exactly what a headless solver should encode: estimate WB, then **reject/clamp any WB move that pushes skin off the I-line**.
- Per-channel **convergence on the parade** is the universal balance read: drag each channel's trace until R/G/B overlap toward a neutral grey axis (beginners-guide 17:57–18:55; best-node-tree 13:04–13:32; mostyn-read-scopes black-guide 08:58–09:11). The new corpus restates this as the literal WB target — Jenkinson: **"get the R/G/B parade circles to line up,"** diagnose the offending channel and pull *only* it (jenkinson-exact-system 12:28–12:56); Batal's before→after objective is **pulling the blue channel up so R/G/B sit closer together** (batal-conquer-color-page 23:11–23:13). colorkit's Shades-of-Gray is a coarse proxy — a **windowed/zone-gated parade balance** (separate shadow vs highlight neutralization) is the demonstrated refinement (the "black-guide" windowed-to-bottom-15% scope, mostyn-read-scopes 07:50–09:33).
- **WB magnitudes are TINY.** The new corpus quantifies a key implementation detail: real WB Offset moves stay **within ±1.5 of the 25.00 neutral** (≈ ±0.06 normalized), e.g. blue −0.72, or R 22.95 / G 24.56 / B 25.65 (jenkinson-exact-system 12:56, 37:04) — so an auto-WB solver should **cap its per-channel move to that small range**.
- **WB-in-highlights via per-channel Gain is the preferred tool.** Multiple colorists prefer a per-channel **Gain** move (multiply on highlights) over Offset for white balance: Wampus notes Offset moves lift+gain together and is "slower/less clean," recommending Gain (wampus-pro-seminar 63:42–65:40); editing-explained-2hr does WB-in-highlights as **warm = +R/−B gain, cool = −R/+B gain** (00:40, 92:32); Benkabouche biases hue with a single-channel Gain pull (B-down→yellow, R-down→cyan, benkabouche-1h-master 07:46/07:55/08:31). A distinct school (editing-explained-2hr) keeps the **wheels neutral and does WB entirely on Temp/Tint** (Temp −560 to cool, 78:30). colorkit's green-anchored per-channel gain already implements the Gain-based school in linear; keep it.
- Warmth via **Blue-offset-down, not a temp slider** (frenchie 12:19): colorkit's green-anchored per-channel gain already does the equivalent in linear; keep it.

**Verdict:** keep Shades-of-Gray as the estimator, but **gate it on skin** (a WB move that worsens the skin-hue angle gets damped) and add a **per-zone (shadow/highlight) neutralization** option. P1.

## 4. Headless scopes are not optional — they are the engine's self-validation and the only way it can know it's right

"Scopes cannot be deceived" / "scopes are ground truth" recurs in every video (beginners-guide 12:41; cullen-master-scopes 08:25; mostyn-read-scopes 00:24). The new corpus is emphatic on this — Qazi: **"trust scopes over the display"** (a $30k reference monitor is "truth," scopes are the affordable substitute, qazi-start-here 02:19/18:35–18:47); Batal calls the **Parade his most-used scope** (batal-conquer-color-page 06:08); Benkabouche **diagnoses log-vs-standard off the Parade** (compressed mid-band = log/no-clip, full-height pinned = crushed/clipped, benkabouche-1h-master 09:19–09:32). colorkit has **no scopes at all** — it cannot self-check a grade, detect clipping, or QC skin. This is the second-biggest gap.

Demonstrated targets the engine should compute per representative frame on a **10-bit 0–1023 scale** and assert against (the **0–1023 gridlines 0/128/256/384/512/640/768/896/1023** are confirmed in qazi-start-here 19:07, batal-conquer-color-page 06:08, benkabouche-1h-master 06:37, neistadt-ultimate-guide 21:06, editing-explained-2hr 63:56; Wampus reads the same as a **0–100 IRE** scale, wampus-pro-seminar 05:30):
- **Black floor off 0, diffuse white ≈896/1023 (~87.5%), nothing pinned at 1023** (beginners-guide 07:09/16:15; mostyn 11:52; film-emulation 16:15). Confirmed: a graded parade reads **peaks ~768–896, bases lifted ~64–128, no clip at 1023, channels aligned** (editing-explained-2hr 70:38). colorkit's soft-clip protects highlights but never *verifies* the result.
- **Clipping is a flat PLATEAU at the rail, not any single pixel touching it.** Wampus defines clipping precisely as a flat line at the very top (blown) or bottom (crushed) of the waveform (wampus-pro-seminar 10:32, 17:22), and Jenkinson cautions **don't flag a small specular spike at 100 — only broad midtone energy pinned** (jenkinson-exact-system 10:34). Keep highlights **just under** the 100 rail for grading room (wampus 41:32–41:40).
- **Parade per-channel convergence** = the balance metric (max R/G/B trace separation → 0).
- **Vectorscope skin lobe on/near the 123° I-line** (validate #2 above). Qazi enumerates the **vectorscope's four jobs**: color balance, shot match, skin-tone protection, saturation-limit monitoring (qazi-start-here 19:39) — a clean checklist of auto-checks for `scopes.py`.
- **Clip detection = % of samples at 0 and at 1023** (2hr-beginner-course "clipping = you've lost all information" 10:55).
- **Configurable reference floor/ceiling** as a cinematic guardrail — Neistadt sets Reference Levels **83/964** so blacks never crush and highlights never clip (neistadt-ultimate-guide 24:10).
- **False-color / IRE bands** for the skin-exposure gate (mostyn-read-scopes; `grading-too-complicated` band-0 = mid-grey 0.391; False Color is a listed Resolve FX, faris-full-course-2026 82:10; per-band vectorscopes with Low/High Range 0.30/0.70 thresholds, neistadt-ultimate-guide 28:28–28:33).

**Engine:** a new `scopes.py` (numpy-only, no GUI) computing parade histograms, a luma waveform summary, a 2D vectorscope hue-mass / centroid, skin-ROI IRE bands, and clip percentages — emitted into the per-shot log + an optional `--validate` JSON sidecar. This makes the develop **falsifiable** and closes the loop the hub keeps asking for. P0/P1.

## 5. The looks are tint-only — add the four texture primitives that actually make "film": halation, grain, vignette, density

The film-emulation evidence is unanimous that **the film look is a STACK of separable, ordered, toggle-able texture nodes — not a LUT** (film-emulation-16-35mm 00:34/16:24; mostyn-perfect-node-tree; 2hr-beginner-course). colorkit's 10 looks are split-tone + s-curve + LGG only — genuinely good *color* character, but they have **no texture**, which is what separates "graded" from "filmic." The demonstrated, parameter-complete primitives:

- **Halation = linear-light, red-weighted highlight bloom**: linearize → Gaussian blur+glow on the **top ~20% luma** (curve flat below a high knee) → recombine with **Add/Screen** (film-emulation 11:36–12:26, 17:56; mostyn glow `Screen` beats Overlay, threshold 0.75, 40:19). The new corpus confirms it as a **dedicated finishing node**: Neistadt has a Glow node (#21) and a separate Halation OFX, used for "beauty/weddings/emotional moments" (neistadt-ultimate-guide 53:59, 54:45); euro-pro-level-64min puts Glow in the finishing tail of its template tree (25:03). colorkit already grades in linear (`tonemap`), so this drops in cleanly as a `stylize` op.
- **Grain = monochrome (sat 0) Overlay noise, mid/shadow-weighted, size ~0.36 / strength ~0.38**, pre-rendered once and tiled (2hr-beginner-course 62:14–62:58; mullins shadows 0.605 > mids 0.287 > **highlights 0.000** to keep speculars clean, 54:44). The new corpus gives a complete second parameter set: Resolve's **Film Grain OFX "35mm 400T" = Composite Overlay, Opacity 0.500, Texture 0.750, Strength 0.284, Softness 0.234** (custom: Texture 0.791, Grain Size 0.721, Strength 0.310) (neistadt-ultimate-guide 52:22, 52:33) — independently confirming the Overlay-noise model and the ~0.3 strength envelope, and that grain is its own late node (#23). Heaviest op → bake a tiled plate, composite at low opacity.
- **Vignette = feathered elliptical radial luma falloff** (every video; frenchie 20:49 Size/Aspect/Pan/Tilt/Soft; 2hr 42:08). The new corpus adds concrete vignette envelopes: an **inverted ellipse darkened by Gamma −0.02..−0.05, surround Gain ~0.92, subject Gain ~+5%** (jenkinson-exact-system 17:08/17:40/29:08), and all four window types with explicit softness values (editing-explained-2hr 53:46–60:20). Trivial in numpy/ffmpeg.
- **Density / subtractive saturation = "deepen, don't electrify"** — the demonstrated pro saturation is **NOT a naive HSV multiply**: Frenchie uses Color-Slice subtractive sat (+14%) "deepens instead of electrifies" (22:28–22:33); `grading-too-complicated` does sat in **HSV S-channel or per-hue density**, never RGB gain (09:38–11:51); Cullen uses a **luma-preserving 3×3 channel matrix** over global sat (mullins 32:12). The new corpus names the exact tools: euro-pro-level-64min runs a **Color Slice / Color Density** stage (subtractive Den / Den.Depth / Sat.Depth per hue-vector, 03:14, 24:30); Benkabouche uses the **Color Warper Hue-Saturation** (per-hue Center/Hue/Sat on −1..1, with skin isolated) for *targeted* density rather than broad sat (benkabouche-1h-master 17:17–17:20); Jenkinson **sets the saturation node's color space to HSV** so the moves behave softly (jenkinson-exact-system 14:05); and Wampus confirms the underlying math — **"saturation is basically a gain"** (a multiplicative chroma gain), with the caveat that an **HSV-mapped node remaps the RGB slots to H/S/V** so a "gain" push can act on a sat/hue axis (wampus-pro-seminar 71:06, 78:28). colorkit's `vibrance` is already chroma-aware and skin-protecting (good), but the **looks' `_saturate` is a naive HSV-style multiply** — upgrade to a density/per-hue model with a protected **Skin** band.

**Engine:** extend `stylize.py` with `halation`, `grain` (baked plate), `vignette`, and a `density`/per-hue-sat op; expose each as a toggle on a look preset (a look becomes an ordered stack, not one cube). The **film-gate border is a compositing overlay, not a grade op** (film-emulation 15:02) — keep it out of the color path. P1 (halation + grain are the high-value pair for "filmic"), P2 (vignette + density).

## 6. Look opacity and the "look-under-the-grade" rule — colorkit already nails this

Strong corroboration of an existing colorkit choice: every film method runs the look at **~50–70% strength under the grade** (film-emulation 50% Key-Output 05:50; mostyn Film-Look Blend 0.321; 2hr Gallery-apply). The new corpus generalizes this into a **universal per-stage strength knob**: Jenkinson uses Resolve's **Key Output Gain (~0.5) as a single "strength" dial for ANY node** — WB correction, secondary mask, *and* creative LUTs, which "almost always read too strong on apply" and get pulled back to **0.5–0.57** (jenkinson-exact-system 16:18, 31:10, 38:27); Neistadt confirms Key Output Gain 1.0 = node-opacity neutral (neistadt-ultimate-guide 11:33); Qazi's one-click look exposes a single **Master Blend** (qazi-start-here 13:11). colorkit's `--look-opacity` default **0.7** and the `bake_look_at_opacity` identity→look lerp are exactly right — **no change, this is a validated decision**, and the corpus says to generalize it to a **per-stage** opacity (see new gap-map row `per-stage-strength-knob`). Note the demonstrated *typical* number is closer to **0.32–0.57** for heavy print/LUT looks; consider lowering the default for the texture-heavy looks once added.

## 7. Saturation must stay luma-safe — colorkit's `apply_to_luminance` is the right architecture

Cullen's single "huge" project flag is **Lum Mix = 0 by default**: saturation/channel moves must NOT drag luminance (cullen-36 25:39/38:28; cullen-contrast). The new corpus shows the *opposite* default convention is also common — **Lum Mix 100.00 (luminance fully coupled)** is the out-of-box Resolve readout in nearly every new video (lenz 08:24; wampus 01:16; jenkinson 00:23; batal 00:21; qazi 00:03; faris 00:24; neistadt 00:02; content-creators-indepth 19:54), and euro-pro-level-64min even shows it at **Lum Mix 0.00** (00:33) — so this remains a genuine **disputed knob** (preserved in §10), not a settled default. The principle Cullen states still holds: when you *want* luma-safe sat, decouple it. colorkit's develop is already luma-safe (contrast/shoulder run through `apply_to_luminance`, vibrance scales chroma around luma) — **validated**. The one place this is violated is the **looks' `_saturate`/split-tone in gamma-encoded 0–1**, which is a per-channel operation that can skew hue/luma. Moving the look-time saturation to the density/luma-preserving model (§5) closes it. P2.

## 8. Shot-matching: the demonstrated metric is hue-MASS overlap + a hero frame + damping — colorkit's mean/std LAB is a weaker proxy

colorkit's Reinhard LAB mean/std transfer toward a median-luma hero (with `--match-alpha 0.8`) is a sound, flicker-safe baseline and matches the hero-shot workflow (beginners-guide 20:26; cullen-master-scopes 09:14). The demonstrated refinements:
- Match metric = **maximize 2D vectorscope hue-mass overlap** between adjacent shots, not just per-channel mean/std (cullen-master-scopes 08:52–11:07). Mean/std can match averages while the hue *distributions* still diverge. The new corpus adds the human-facing methods: **match on skin position on the vectorscope + a reference-still split read as a waveform step** (jenkinson-exact-system 31:53/32:05/33:00, where a "warm/cool nudge" = an Offset toward the reference's skin angle), and Resolve's **Image-Wipe dual-viewer / diagonal-split / alpha-blend** plus **X-Rite ColorChecker auto-match** (euro-pro-level-64min 21:07–21:27, 23:32).
- **Group-then-match, program-first.** Qazi's strong addition: before grading anything, throw the whole timeline into **Lightbox**, read the program holistically, **cluster shots into scene/location groups**, flag outliers, and grade **group-wise** rather than per-clip in isolation (qazi-start-here 16:10–16:48); Neistadt does the same Lightbox consistency pass (neistadt-ultimate-guide 02:10, 14:52). And the cheap propagation primitive is **Paste Attributes → Color Correction** to push one hero grade across a shot family (content-creators-indepth 32:20; neistadt PowerGrade reuse 12:56). **Conform mixed frame rates first** so multicam cuts together before color matching (euro-pro-level-64min 09:50–10:23).
- **Exclude saturated set-dressing** (red flowers/costumes throw the stats) and **judge on the skin lobe** (cullen-master-scopes 07:44; cullen-36). colorkit matches on the whole-frame LAB stats — a **skin-weighted or set-dressing-masked** match would be more robust for interview cutaways.
- **Damping < 1.0 / partial convergence** to avoid over-correction (cullen-master-scopes) — colorkit's `match_alpha=0.8` already implements this. Validated.
- **Temporal averaging** of the scope read over several frames before committing (cullen-master-scopes 08:18; mostyn; **loop-the-clip-while-grading** neistadt-ultimate-guide 02:10) — colorkit uses ONE median-brightness rep frame; averaging a few frames' stats would reduce single-frame noise. P2.

## 9. Input transform / CST sandwich — colorkit's approach is sound for its single-camera reality

The universal pro contract is the **CST sandwich**: decode camera log/gamut → wide working space → output transform (best-node-tree; grading-too-complicated; mullins; all of them). colorkit's reality is narrower and the design fits it: it grades in **sRGB-linear** (not DWG), applies the **Insta360 I-Log→Rec709 BT1886 input LUT** auto-detected by filename (`color.py` `_detect_input_lut`), and HDR HLG/PQ tonemap first. For a **single-camera creator**, sRGB-linear is a defensible working space and the I-Log LUT is the per-camera input CST. The demonstrated gap is **only-the-input-transform-changes-per-camera** (best-node-tree) — if phone/other-camera b-roll becomes common, a small **camera→input-LUT lookup** (iPhone = Rec.709 passthrough, etc., best-node-tree 16:36/21:18) is the clean extension. Lower priority given the footage profile. P3.

## 10. Disputes preserved as tunable knobs (do not hard-code one side)

- **Output gamma 2.2 vs 2.4** — Mullins delivers **2.2** ("for the longest time I used 2.4," mullins 02:59); best-node-tree/cullen use **2.4** (calibrated monitor). For IG/SDR web delivery, **2.2 is the better default** but expose it. (colorkit's sRGB OETF ≈ 2.2-ish — fine; flag if a 2.4 path is ever needed.)
- **Contrast tool: pivoted-slider vs Gain+Offset vs curve** — `grading-too-complicated` prefers **Gain+Offset over the contrast slider** (08:34); Cullen proves the **S-curve sigmoid** (cullen-36 27:44); Mullins uses a **gentle near-linear curve with a soft toe, not a hard S** (11:10). colorkit's single pivoted-power contrast is the simplest valid choice — keep it, but the **soft-toe/lifted-toe filmic curve** is the demonstrated film signature (mullins 43:45; film-emulation 16:15) and belongs as an optional develop curve, not just in the looks.
- **Pivot bias** (highlight-protect low vs shadow-protect high) — expose, don't fix.
- **Grain weighting** — shadows-only (mullins) vs mid-weighted (2hr) — tunable per look.
- **Skin resting point** — on-line vs slightly-green-filmic — tunable, defaulting to a hair under the I-line for the filmic read (mostyn 04:55).
